# Inside the Gluten-Free Protein & Fibre Roti Mix: Wheat-Free, Everyday

Anyone who has tried to make a wheat-free roti knows the problem. Wheat owes its magic to gluten — the stretchy protein network that lets an atta dough be kneaded, rolled thin, and puffed on the tava without tearing. Take the gluten away, and most flours simply crumble: the dough cracks at the edges, refuses to roll into a neat round, and the roti falls apart before it reaches the plate. Our **Gluten-Free Protein & Fibre Roti Mix** is engineered to solve exactly that. It is a purposeful blend of millets, pulse flours, heritage red rice, and natural binders that rolls, folds, and stays soft — a wheat-free flatbread that behaves like the everyday roti you already know.

## The everyday roti, made wheat-free

The roti is the quiet workhorse of the Indian meal — the soft flatbread that shows up at lunch and dinner across most of the country, torn and folded around dal, sabzi, and curry. For most households it means wheat atta, but India also has a long and living tradition of grain flatbreads made without wheat at all: jowar bhakri and rotla across Maharashtra and Karnataka, bajra rotla and bhakri across Gujarat and Rajasthan, and rice-flour breads down south. These are not novelties; they are ancestral everyday breads, made from the millets and grains that have fed the drier and warmer regions for centuries.

The catch is that these traditional wheat-free breads are famously tricky to roll. Bhakri and rotla are usually patted out by hand rather than rolled with a pin, precisely because the dough has no gluten to stretch. Our Roti Mix takes the flavours and grains of that heritage and adds a small amount of quiet structural help — natural binders that let a wheat-free dough be kneaded and rolled the ordinary way. The tradition of the grain flatbread stays intact; the frustration of a dough that cracks does not. This is a familiar meal, reimagined so it fits into everyday cooking without any special technique.

## What's in it, and why

Every ingredient earns its place. Here is the full composition by weight:

- **Jowar / sorghum (25%)** — the mild, gluten-free base
- **Roasted Bengal gram (20%)** — protein, fibre, and flavour
- **Pearl millet / bajra (15%)** — fibre, minerals, and traditional grain value
- **Sprouted green gram flour (15%)** — plant protein and differentiation
- **Traditional red rice (12%)** — dough handling and a lighter texture
- **Tapioca starch (7%)** — flexibility and softness, less cracking
- **Psyllium husk (4%)** — binding and water retention for rolling
- **Flaxseed (2%)** — fibre, healthy fats, and moisture

### An ingredient-by-ingredient walkthrough

**Jowar (sorghum)** is the base of the blend at 25%. It is a mild, naturally gluten-free grain with a long history as a bhakri and rotla flour, which makes it the natural foundation here. Its gentle flavour lets the other grains and pulses come through, and it gives the roti its familiar, wholesome character. Our guide to jowar covers this grain in more depth if you would like to know it on its own.

**Roasted Bengal gram** at 20% is a pulse flour that brings protein, fibre, and a lovely nutty, roasted flavour. Bengal gram (chana) flour is a classic in Indian cooking, and roasting it deepens the taste — it is a big part of why this roti has flavour rather than being merely a neutral carrier.

**Pearl millet (bajra)** at 15% is one of India's great traditional flatbread grains, the heart of the bajra rotla eaten across the west. It contributes fibre, minerals, and that traditional grain value, adding a warm, earthy note to the blend. Our guide to bajra explores this grain further.

**Sprouted green gram flour** at 15% is a point of difference in this blend. Using a sprouted pulse flour adds plant protein and gives the mix a distinct character, contributing to the protein-and-fibre profile that defines the product.

**Traditional red rice** at 12% is here for dough handling and a lighter texture. Red rice flour helps the dough behave and roll more smoothly, lightening what could otherwise be a dense, heavy bread and improving the finished softness.

**Tapioca starch** at 7% is one of the two quiet structural workers in the blend. Tapioca brings flexibility and softness and reduces cracking — it is part of what allows a gluten-free dough to be rolled and folded rather than crumbling. It is a small share, but it does outsized work.

**Psyllium husk** at 4% is the other key binder. Psyllium absorbs and holds water, forming a gel that gives the dough cohesion and stretch — the closest a gluten-free mix comes to mimicking the binding role gluten plays in wheat. This is what lets the dough hold together for rolling and keeps the finished roti pliable.

**Flaxseed** at 2% adds fibre and healthy fats, and it helps with moisture retention, contributing to a dough that stays supple. Flaxseed is a declared allergen, so it is worth noting for anyone with sensitivities.

### Why the formulation is built this way

This blend solves a structural problem, and its design reflects that. First, **grain diversity and a pulse pairing** carry the nutrition: jowar, bajra, and red rice bring the grains, while roasted Bengal gram and sprouted green gram flour bring the plant protein and much of the fibre. Second, and crucially, **the binders make it work**: tapioca starch and psyllium husk do the quiet structural job that gluten does in wheat, holding the dough together so it can be kneaded and rolled, while flaxseed helps retain moisture for softness. The millets and pulse flours carry the fibre and protein; tapioca and psyllium do the structural work that lets a gluten-free dough behave. This is the difference between a roti that tears and one that folds neatly around a curry — everyday nutrition on transparent ingredients, with none of the usual wheat-free frustration.

## How to make it

The whole point of this mix is that you can make it the ordinary way, without patting the dough by hand.

1. **Knead.** Add warm water gradually to the flour and knead into a smooth, pliable dough. Warm water helps the binders hydrate and the dough come together.
2. **Rest.** Cover and let the dough rest for 10 to 15 minutes. This step matters — it gives the psyllium time to hydrate fully and makes the dough far easier to roll.
3. **Roll.** Divide into balls. Roll each between two sheets of parchment or on a lightly dusted surface into a round. Rolling between sheets is the easiest way to get a neat, even roti with a gluten-free dough.
4. **Cook.** Cook on a hot tava until soft with light brown spots, turning to cook both sides. A little oil or ghee brushed on keeps the roti supple.

### Variations and tips for handling the dough

- **Dough consistency:** Aim for smooth and pliable, not sticky. If it feels dry or crumbly, add a little more warm water and knead again; if it is too wet, dust in a little of the mix.
- **Let it rest:** Do not skip the rest. A properly rested dough rolls far more easily and cracks far less, because the psyllium needs time to do its work.
- **Rolling:** If you find rolling tricky at first, use two sheets of parchment — it removes almost all the difficulty of a gluten-free dough.
- **Keeping them soft:** Brush the hot roti lightly with oil or ghee and stack them covered with a cloth to keep them soft and warm until serving.

## Serving suggestions and meal occasions

This roti fits wherever an ordinary roti would. Serve it at **lunch or dinner** with dal, sabzi, or any curry — it folds and tears the way a flatbread should, so it works as a spoon and a wrapper both. It suits **family meals**, where one wheat-free bread on the table can serve everyone rather than cooking separately for different diets. And because it holds together and stays soft, it is a practical **tiffin** bread, packing well for lunchboxes and travelling without turning brittle. Roll a smaller, softer version for children, or a slightly larger one to fold around a generous helping at dinner.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Roti Mix vegan, wheat-free, and gluten-free?**
It is vegan and wheat-free, with no added sugar and no maida, and it is formulated to be gluten-free. As noted on the pack, its gluten-free status is subject to final validated cross-contamination control and testing. If you finish the rotis with ghee they are no longer strictly vegan, so use oil to keep them plant-based.

**Does it contain allergens?**
Yes — it contains pulses (roasted Bengal gram and sprouted green gram flour) and flaxseed, both of which are declared allergens. Please read the pack carefully if you have any food sensitivities.

**Why does gluten-free roti usually crack, and how does this mix fix it?**
Wheat dough holds together because of gluten, which gluten-free flours lack. This mix uses natural binders — tapioca starch and psyllium husk — that hold water and give the dough cohesion, so it can be kneaded and rolled rather than crumbling. Letting the dough rest so the psyllium hydrates is the key step.

**How long does it take, and do I need special equipment?**
Kneading takes a few minutes, then the dough rests for 10 to 15 minutes before rolling and cooking each roti on a hot tava. No special equipment is needed, though rolling between two sheets of parchment makes it easier.

**How should I store the mix, and can children and older adults eat these rotis?**
Keep the dry mix in an airtight container in a cool, dry place away from moisture and sunlight, and use it by the best-before date on the pack. The finished rotis are a familiar, everyday bread that may suit many households, including families with children and older adults — roll them thinner and softer and brush with a little oil to keep them tender. It is a food, not a medical product, so please follow professional advice for any specific dietary requirement.
